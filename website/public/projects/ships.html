Ships!

Summary

This game is a 2-4 player party game in which players control a pirate ship and must shoot cannons to destroy other ships. The niche to the game is that to reload the cannonballs, the player must spin a joystick in rotation. The more consecutive spins without fire grant more ammo per spin. They can fire the cannonballs from either side of the ship, depleting 15 rounds per trigger pull a maximum of four times. There's and island that can be shot over at the cost of a chance a ball hits the ground and flies uncontrollably in the air. A player wins when they are the last boat standing.

This game has an alternate version where two people control the same boat, one person steers and the other shoots and reloads.

About

Background

I wanted to make a fun party game to play with my friends in the summer. The theme had to be fun for everyone and able to be made with my 3D modeling skills. I landed on the idea that pirate ships would be a perfect fit and begin thinking about gameplay elements. I started a blank document and filled it with bulleted ideas. I then shaved ideas down to get to core elements of the game. I decided a 2D top down with joystick controllers would be the best start for a party game. I then thought something interactive would also be a must to make the game engaging. Once I had the idea built in my head, I booted up Unity3D.

The game I prototyped looked similar to the game I have now. It was 2-4 minimalistic ships with UI around them. I did this because I wanted everyone to just be able to focus on their ship. Originally, I had random buttons display to indicate what the player should press to reload a cannonball. It was color coded and position relative. (So the b button was red and to the right like the targeted controllers) I did a test run with my dad who has used xbox controllers before, but still needed to look at the controller time to time. I whooped him in many of the games due to my familiarity with controllers. But while it was fun to beat him, this wasn't the purpose of the game and I wasn't the target audience. I then switched it to the current joystick spinning system with indicators for the direction needed to spin. This worked perfectly as players didn't need to lift their thumbs but still felt pressured to reload quicker than an opponent.

Another issue was steering. I always imagined the best method was the ship points to the direction of the left joystick. When playing that first test run with my dad however, it didn't feel natural. He was use to steering with the left stick. I made a change to see how I liked steering the ship with only left and right and I felt indifferent about it. I kept the changes when I tested a later version with my friends who were familiar with controllers and different genres of games and they prefered the original way, so I reverted it back. Controlling choices are definitely a feature I will add later to please both groups.

Finally, with gameplay out of the way, I created extra effects like explosions, cannonball trails, and planks flying off the ships when they got hit. With a bit of music, a custom water shader, and my own sound effects, the game became what I wanted: Fun to quickly play with anyone.
